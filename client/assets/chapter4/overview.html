Chapter 4: Quadratic Equations
1. Introduction
A quadratic equation in the variable x is an equation of the form ax² + bx + c = 0, where a, b, c are real numbers and a ≠ 0. Quadratic equations arise in many real-world situations, especially those involving areas and projectiles.
When we find the values of x that satisfy the equation, we are finding the roots (or zeros) of the quadratic equation. A quadratic equation can have at most two real roots.
2. Definitions
Quadratic Equation: An equation where the highest power of the variable is 2. The standard form is ax² + bx + c = 0, where a ≠ 0.
Roots/Zeros: The values of the variable (x) for which the equation becomes zero. A quadratic equation has two roots, which may be real or imaginary.
Discriminant (D): The value b² - 4ac, which determines the nature of the roots of a quadratic equation.
Completing the Square: A method used to solve a quadratic equation by changing the form of the equation so that the left side is a perfect square trinomial.
Quadratic Formula: A formula that provides the solution(s) to a quadratic equation in terms of its coefficients a, b, and c.
3. Formulas and Nature of Roots
Quadratic Formula:
The roots of a quadratic equation ax² + bx + c = 0 are given by:
x = -b ± √D2a
Where D = b² - 4ac is the Discriminant.
Nature of Roots based on D:
Case 1: D > 0
Two distinct real roots exist.
Roots are -b + √D2a and -b - √D2a.
Case 2: D = 0
Two equal real roots exist (Real and Coincident).
Roots are -b2a, -b2a.
Case 3: D < 0
No real roots exist (Roots are imaginary).
4. Chapter Crux
The heart of this chapter is understanding that not all equations have real solutions, and we can predict this without solving the equation completely using the Discriminant.
1. Solving Methods:
  • Factorisation: Best when the quadratic expression can be easily factored into (px + q)(rx + s). Ideally used when D is a perfect square.
  • Quadratic Formula: The universal method. Works for any quadratic equation regardless of whether it's factorable or not.
  • Completing the Square: Useful for deriving the quadratic formula and in geometric contexts, though less commonly used for quick solving.
2. Word Problem Strategy:
  • Identify the unknown quantity and let it be 'x'.
  • Translate the given conditions into a mathematical equation involving x and usually its square (x²).
  • Form the standard quadratic equation ax² + bx + c = 0.
  • Solve for x and discard any values that don't make sense in context (e.g., negative length or age).
5. Summary
In this chapter, we delved into Quadratic Equations, which are equations of the second degree. We started with the standard form ax² + bx + c = 0.
We explored multiple ways to find the roots of these equations: by factorising into linear terms, by completing the square, and by using the powerful Quadratic Formula. We learned that a quadratic equation can have two distinct real roots, two equal real roots, or no real roots at all.
The key takeaway is the concept of the Discriminant (b² - 4ac), which acts as a quick test to determine the nature of the roots without actually solving the equation. This chapter bridges the gap between simple linear relationships and more complex, curved relationships found in areas, physics, and optimization problems.
