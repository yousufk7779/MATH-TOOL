Chapter 2: Polynomials
Introduction
We encounter polynomials in various fields of mathematics and science to model real-world phenomena. In this chapter, we explore the geometrical meaning of zeroes of a polynomial and the relationship between zeroes and coefficients. This forms the foundation for algebra and calculus.
Chapter Crux
The core essence of this chapter lies in understanding two things:
  • Geometrical Meaning: Zeroes of a polynomial p(x) are simply the x-coordinates where the graph of y = p(x) crosses the X-axis.
  • Algebraic Relationship: There is a direct link between the sum and product of zeroes and the coefficients of the polynomial.
Key Points
  • A polynomial of degree n can have at most n zeroes.
  • The graph of a linear polynomial (degree 1) is a straight line.
  • The graph of a quadratic polynomial (degree 2) is a parabola opening upwards (a > 0) or downwards (a < 0).
  • A quadratic polynomial can intersect the X-axis at at most 2 points.
Key Definitions
  • Polynomial: An algebraic expression with variables having non-negative integer exponents.
  • Degree: The highest power of the variable in the polynomial.
  • Linear Polynomial: A polynomial of degree 1 (e.g., 2x - 3).
  • Quadratic Polynomial: A polynomial of degree 2 (e.g., x² + 5x + 6).
  • Cubic Polynomial: A polynomial of degree 3.
  • Zero of a Polynomial: A real number k is a zero of p(x) if p(k) = 0. Geometrically, it is the x-intercept.
Important Formulas
1. Standard Form of Quadratic Polynomial:
ax² + bx + c (where a ≠ 0)
2. Relationship between Zeroes and Coefficients:
If α and β are zeroes of ax² + bx + c:
Sum of zeroes (α + β) = -b / a
Product of zeroes (αβ) = c / a
3. Forming a Quadratic Polynomial:
If sum (S) and product (P) of zeroes are given:
k [ x² - (Sum)x + (Product) ]
k [ x² - (α+β)x + αβ ]
Summary
In this chapter, we learned that the zeroes of a polynomial are the points where its graph cuts the x-axis. We established that a quadratic polynomial has at most 2 zeroes. Most importantly, we derived the relationship between the zeroes (roots) and the coefficients, allowing us to build polynomials if we know their roots, or check the validity of roots without plugging them in directly.
