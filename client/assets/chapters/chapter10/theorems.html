Theorem 10.1: The tangent at any point of a circle is perpendicular to the radius through the point of contact.
Proof:
Given: A circle with center O and a tangent XY at a point P.
To Prove: OP is perpendicular to XY (OP ⊥ XY).
Construction: Take a point Q on tangent XY, other than P. Join OQ. Let OQ intersect the circle at point R.
Proof:
We have a point Q on tangent XY (other than contact point P).
Therefore, point Q must lie outside the circle.
(If Q was inside, XY would be a secant, not a tangent).
Since Q is outside, the distance OQ is greater than the radius of the circle.
So, length OQ > length OP (since OP is the radius).
This is true for every point on line XY, except point P.
It means OP is the shortest distance from center O to the line XY.
We know that the shortest distance from a point to a line is the perpendicular distance.
Therefore, OP ⊥ XY.
Final Conclusion: Hence, the tangent at any point of a circle is perpendicular to the radius through the point of contact.
Theorem 10.2: The lengths of tangents drawn from an external point to a circle are equal.
Proof:
Given: A circle with center O. An external point P. Two tangents PQ and PR drawn from P to the circle.
To Prove: Length PQ = Length PR.
Construction: Join center O to points P, Q, and R.
Proof:
PQ is a tangent at Q and OQ is the radius.
According to Theorem 10.1 (Radius is perpendicular to tangent):
\(\angle OQP = 90^\circ\).
Similarly, PR is a tangent at R and OR is the radius.
So, \(\angle ORP = 90^\circ\).
Now, consider the right-angled triangles \(\triangle OQP\) and \(\triangle ORP\).
\(OQ = OR\) (Radii of the same circle are equal).
\(OP = OP\) (Common hypotenuse side).
By RHS Congruence Rule (Right angle - Hypotenuse - Side):
\(\triangle OQP \cong \triangle ORP\).
Since the triangles are congruent, their corresponding parts are equal (CPCT).
Therefore, \(PQ = PR\).
Final Conclusion: Hence Proved.
