Chapter 9: Some Applications of Trigonometry
1. Introduction
In the previous chapter, we studied trigonometric ratios. In this chapter, we will explore some ways in which trigonometry is used in the life around us.
Trigonometry is one of the most ancient subjects studied by scholars all over the world. Astronomers used it to calculate distances from the Earth to the planets and stars.
We will see how trigonometry is used to finding the heights and distances of various objects like towers, mountains, buildings, etc., without actually measuring them.
2. Heights and Distances
To solve problems involving heights and distances, we need to understand the following terms:
Line of Sight: The line drawn from the eye of an observer to the point in the object viewed by the observer.
Horizontal Level: The line drawn horizontally through the eye of the observer.
Angle of Elevation: The angle formed by the line of sight with the horizontal when the point being viewed is ABOVE the horizontal level (i.e., when we raise our head to look at the object).
Angle of Depression: The angle formed by the line of sight with the horizontal when the point being viewed is BELOW the horizontal level (i.e., when we lower our head to look at the object).
3. Key Concepts
  • The angle of elevation of an object as viewed by an observer is the angle between the horizontal line and the line of sight.
  • If the observer moves towards the object (e.g., a tower), the angle of elevation increases.
  • If the observer moves away from the object, the angle of elevation decreases.
  • For the same height, if the distance is doubled, the angle of elevation decreases but does not become half.
  • The angle of depression of a point on the ground from the top of a tower is numerically equal to the angle of elevation of the top of the tower from that point on the ground (Alternate Interior Angles).
4. Trigonometric Ratios Recap
For a right-angled triangle with angle θ:
  • sin θ = Opposite Side / Hypotenuse
  • cos θ = Adjacent Side / Hypotenuse
  • tan θ = Opposite Side / Adjacent Side
5. Important Values to Remember
Most problems in this chapter involve angles of 30°, 45°, and 60°. Memorize these values:
tan 30° = 1/√3 ≈ 0.577
tan 45° = 1
tan 60° = √3 ≈ 1.732
sin 30° = 1/2
sin 45° = 1/√2
sin 60° = √3/2
cos 30° = √3/2
cos 45° = 1/√2
cos 60° = 1/2
6. Steps to Solve Problems
1. Read the problem carefully and draw a neat, labeled diagram. Represent the height (tower, building) by a vertical line and the distance by a horizontal line.
2. Mark the given angles of elevation or depression. Remember, angle of depression is marked with the horizontal line at the top.
3. Identify the right-angled triangle(s) formed.
4. Choose the appropriate trigonometric ratio (usually tan θ, sometimes sin θ or cos θ) that relates the known side to the unknown side.
5. Form an equation and solve for the unknown value.
