Chapter 3: Pair of Linear Equations in Two Variables
1. Introduction
A linear equation in two variables is an equation of the form ax + by + c = 0, where a, b, and c are real numbers, and a and b are not both zero. This chapter focuses on the "pair" of such equations, meaning two linear equations in the same two variables considered together.
Geometrically, a linear equation represents a straight line. When we have a pair of linear equations, we are looking at two lines in the same plane. The solution to the pair corresponds to the point(s) common to both lines.
2. Definitions
Linear Equation: An algebraic equation where the highest degree of the variable is 1. Its graph is always a straight line.
System of Equations: A set of two or more equations with the same variables.
Solution: A pair of values (x, y) that satisfies both equations simultaneously.
Consistent System: A system of equations that has at least one solution (either a unique solution or infinitely many solutions).
Inconsistent System: A system of equations that has no solution (the lines are parallel).
Dependent System: A consistent system that has infinitely many solutions (the lines coincide).
3. Formulas and Conditions
Standard Form:
Equation 1: a₁x + b₁y + c₁ = 0
Equation 2: a₂x + b₂y + c₂ = 0
Condition 1: Intersecting Lines (Unique Solution)
a₁a₂ ≠ b₁b₂
System is Consistent.
Condition 2: Coincident Lines (Infinitely Many Solutions)
a₁a₂ = b₁b₂ = c₁c₂
System is Consistent (Dependent).
Condition 3: Parallel Lines (No Solution)
a₁a₂ = b₁b₂ ≠ c₁c₂
System is Inconsistent.
4. Chapter Crux
The crux of this chapter lies in understanding the relationship between the algebraic representation and the geometric representation of lines.
1. Graphical Representation:
  • If lines intersect at one point → Customary unique solution.
  • If lines are parallel → No solution.
  • If lines overlap exactly → Infinitely many solutions.
2. Algebraic Methods:
  • Substitution Method: Solve for one variable and substitute it into the other equation.
  • Elimination Method: Equalize coefficients to eliminate one variable by addition or subtraction.
  • Cross-Multiplication Method: A direct formula-based approach (often optional but useful).
Mastering the conditions for consistency (a₁a₂...) is critical for solving questions quickly without solving the entire system.
5. Summary
In this chapter, we explored the world of "Pair of Linear Equations in Two Variables". We learned that every linear equation in two variables represents a straight line on a Cartesian plane.
We discovered that a pair of such equations can behave in three ways: they intersect, they are parallel, or they coincide. We can predict this behavior simply by comparing the ratios of their coefficients a₁a₂, b₁b₂, and c₁c₂.
Finally, we equipped ourselves with powerful algebraic tools—Substitution and Elimination—to find the exact values of variables, enabling us to solve a wide range of real-world problems involving ages, fractions, speed, and costs.
