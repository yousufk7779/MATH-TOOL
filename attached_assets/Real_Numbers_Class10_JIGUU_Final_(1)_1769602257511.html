Chapter 1: Real Numbers

Class 10 - NCERT

1. Introduction

In Class IX, you explored the world of real numbers and encountered irrational numbers. In this chapter, we continue our discussion on real numbers with two very important properties of positive integers - Euclid's Division Algorithm and the Fundamental Theorem of Arithmetic. We will use these concepts to find HCF and LCM of numbers, prove the irrationality of numbers like √2, √3, √5, and explore when the decimal expansion of a rational number is terminating or non-terminating repeating.

2. Definitions
  • Real Numbers: The collection of all rational and irrational numbers. Denoted by R.
  • Natural Numbers: Counting numbers 1, 2, 3, 4, ... Denoted by N.
  • Whole Numbers: Natural numbers including 0, i.e., 0, 1, 2, 3, ... Denoted by W.
  • Integers: All positive and negative whole numbers including 0. Denoted by Z.
  • Rational Numbers: Numbers that can be written in the form pq where p and q are integers and q ≠ 0. Denoted by Q.
  • Irrational Numbers: Numbers that cannot be written in the form pq. Examples: √2, √3, π.
  • Prime Numbers: Numbers greater than 1 with exactly two factors - 1 and itself. Examples: 2, 3, 5, 7, 11...
  • Composite Numbers: Numbers greater than 1 with more than two factors. Examples: 4, 6, 8, 9...
  • Co-prime Numbers: Two numbers whose HCF is 1. Example: 15 and 28.
3. Key Points
  • Every composite number can be expressed as a product of primes uniquely (Fundamental Theorem of Arithmetic).
  • HCF = Product of smallest powers of common prime factors.
  • LCM = Product of greatest powers of all prime factors.
  • For any two positive integers a and b: HCF(a, b) × LCM(a, b) = a × b.
  • √2, √3, √5 and √p (where p is prime) are irrational numbers.
  • Sum or difference of a rational and an irrational number is irrational.
  • Product or quotient of a non-zero rational and an irrational number is irrational.
  • If a prime p divides a², then p also divides a.
4. Formulas Used in This Chapter
Euclid's Division Lemma a = bq + r, where 0 ≤ r < b
Fundamental Theorem Every composite number = Product of prime factors (unique)
HCF × LCM HCF(a, b) × LCM(a, b) = a × b
HCF (Prime Factorisation) Product of smallest powers of common prime factors
LCM (Prime Factorisation) Product of greatest powers of all prime factors
Theorem 1.2 If p divides a², then p divides a (p is prime)
5. Crux of the Chapter

Core Concept: This chapter establishes the foundation of number theory through two theorems - the Fundamental Theorem of Arithmetic ensures every composite number has a unique prime factorisation. This uniqueness is then used to prove irrationality of numbers like √2, √3, √5 using proof by contradiction.

Exam Focus: Questions on prime factorisation, HCF-LCM using prime factorisation method, and proofs of irrationality are frequently asked. Remember: For HCF, take smallest powers; for LCM, take greatest powers.

Q.5 Check whether 6ⁿ can end with the digit 0 for any natural number n.
Solution:
For a number to end with digit 0, it must be divisible by 10.
10 = 2 × 5
A number ends with 0 only if it has both 2 and 5 as prime factors
Now, 6ⁿ = (2 × 3)ⁿ = 2ⁿ × 3ⁿ
The prime factorisation of 6ⁿ contains only 2 and 3.
There is no factor of 5 in 6ⁿ. Fundamental Theorem of Arithmetic
Since 5 is not a factor of 6ⁿ, it cannot end with digit 0.
No, 6ⁿ cannot end with digit 0 for any natural number n.

∴ 7 × 11 × 13 + 13 is composite.
For 7 × 6 × 5 × 4 × 3 × 2 × 1 + 5:
= 7! + 5 = 5040 + 5 = 5045
= 5(7 × 6 × 4 × 3 × 2 × 1 + 1) Taking 5 common
= 5(1008 + 1)
= 5 × 1009
Since 5045 = 5 × 1009, it has factors other than 1 and itself.
Both are composite numbers as they can be expressed as product of factors other than 1 and themselves.
Q.7 There is a circular path around a sports field. Sonia takes 18 minutes to drive one round of the field, while Ravi takes 12 minutes for the same. Suppose they both start at the same point and at the same time, and go in the same direction. After how many minutes will they meet again at the starting point?
Solution:
Sonia takes 18 minutes for one round.
Ravi takes 12 minutes for one round.
They will meet again at the starting point after LCM(18, 12) minutes.
LCM gives the least time when both complete whole rounds
Prime factorisation:
18 = 2 × 3²
12 = 2² × 3
LCM(18, 12) = 2² × 3² = 4 × 9 = 36 Greatest powers of all primes
They will meet again at the starting point after 36 minutes.
EXERCISE 1.2
Q.1 Prove that √5 is irrational.
Solution:
Assume √5 is rational.
√5 = ab (in lowest form)
Squaring:
a² = 5b²
So a is divisible by 5 → let a = 5k.
Substitute:
25k² = 5b² ⇒ b² = 5k²
So b is also divisible by 5 — contradiction (since fraction was in lowest form).
√5 is irrational.
Q.2 Prove that 3 + 2√5 is irrational.
Solution:
Assume 3 + 2√5 is rational.
Then:
2√5 = (3 + 2√5) − 3
So 2√5 is rational → divide by 2:
√5 is rational
But we already know √5 is irrational.
Contradiction.
3 + 2√5 is irrational.
Q.3 Prove that the following are irrationals:
Q.3(i) 1√2
Solution (Proof by Contradiction):
Step 1: Assume, to the contrary, that 1√2 is rational.
Then 1√2 = ab, where a, b are coprime integers, b ≠ 0.
Proof by Contradiction Method
Step 2: Rearranging:
√2 = ba Cross multiplying
Step 3: Since a and b are integers, ba is rational.
This means √2 is rational.
Step 4: But we know √2 is irrational (Theorem 1.3)!
This is a contradiction.
∴ 1√2 is irrational. (Hence Proved)
Q.3(ii) 7√5
Solution (Proof by Contradiction):
Step 1: Assume, to the contrary, that 7√5 is rational.
Then 7√5 = ab, where a, b are coprime integers, b ≠ 0.
Proof by Contradiction Method
Step 2: Rearranging:
√5 = a/(7b) Dividing both sides by 7
Step 3: Since a, b are integers and 7 is an integer, a/(7b) is rational.
This means √5 is rational.
Step 4: But we know √5 is irrational (proved in Q.1)!
This is a contradiction.
∴ 7√5 is irrational. (Hence Proved)

Solution (Proof by Contradiction):
Step 1: Assume, to the contrary, that 6 + √2 is rational.

Example 5 Prove that √3 is irrational.
Solution (Proof by Contradiction):
Step 1: Assume, to the contrary, that √3 is rational.
Then √3 = ab, where a and b are coprime integers, b ≠ 0.
Proof by Contradiction Method
Step 2: Squaring both sides:
3 = a²b²
∴ 3b² = a² Cross multiplying
This means 3 divides a².
Step 3: By Theorem 1.2, if 3 divides a², then 3 divides a.
So we can write a = 3c for some integer c.
Step 4: Substituting a = 3c in 3b² = a²:
3b² = 9c²
∴ b² = 3c² Dividing by 3
This means 3 divides b².
∴ 3 divides b (by Theorem 1.2)
Step 5: So 3 divides both a and b.
But this contradicts that a and b are coprime!
∴ √3 is irrational. (Hence Proved)

IMPORTANT THEOREMS
Theorem 1.1 (Euclid's Division Lemma)
Statement:
For any positive integers a and b, there exist unique integers q and r such that:
a = bq + r, where 0 ≤ r < b
Simple Meaning:
When we divide a by b, we get quotient q and remainder r.
Remainder is always less than divisor (r < b).
Example: 17 = 5 × 3 + 2 (here a=17, b=5, q=3, r=2)
Theorem 1.2 (Prime Factor Property)
Statement:
If p is a prime number and p divides a², then p divides a.
Proof:
Let a = p₁ × p₂ × ... × pₙ (prime factorisation)
Then a² = p₁² × p₂² × ... × pₙ²
If prime p divides a², then p must be one of p₁, p₂, ..., pₙ
∴ p divides a.
Example: If 5 divides 15², then 5 divides 15 ✓
Theorem 1.3 (Irrationality of √2)
Statement:
√2 is irrational.
Proof:
Assume √2 is rational.
√2 = ab (in lowest form)
Squaring: 2 = a²b² → a² = 2b²
So 2 divides a² → 2 divides a. Let a = 2k.
Then 4k² = 2b² → b² = 2k²
So 2 divides b² → 2 divides b.
But then 2 divides both a and b — contradiction!
∴ √2 is irrational.

Chapter Summary

1. The Fundamental Theorem of Arithmetic: Every composite number can be expressed (factorised) as a product of primes, and this factorisation is unique, apart from the order in which the prime factors occur.

2. If p is a prime and p divides a², then p divides a, where a is a positive integer.

3. √2, √3, √5 are irrational numbers (proved using contradiction).

4. HCF of two or more numbers = Product of smallest powers of common prime factors.

5. LCM of two or more numbers = Product of greatest powers of all prime factors.

6. For any two positive integers a and b: HCF(a, b) × LCM(a, b) = a × b.

7. Sum or difference of a rational and an irrational number is irrational.

8. Product or quotient of a non-zero rational and an irrational number is irrational.
